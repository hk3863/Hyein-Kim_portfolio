NYU Community Dashboard

A centralized campus dashboard concept that brings schedules, shuttles, dining, events, and quick links into one responsive NYU home base.

The project started from a familiar student frustration: essential campus information lives across Albert, Brightspace, NYU Mobile, email, shuttle updates, and separate event pages. I wanted to turn that fragmented routine into one customizable dashboard that feels calm, useful, and personal from the first screen.

This was one of the projects that made UX feel real to me. I enjoyed testing whether the structure made sense, hearing feedback, and iterating until the interface felt clearer and more useful.

01

Overview

NYU Community Dashboard is a UI prototype for students who routinely jump across multiple campus tools just to answer simple daily questions: What classes do I have today? When is the next shuttle? What is open to eat? What events matter to me?

Instead of treating the problem as one more student portal, I framed the dashboard as a personal command center. The goal was to reduce switching, surface the right information quickly, and let each student shape the interface around their own routine.

02

Problem and opportunity

The starting point was fragmentation. Schedules, course platforms, dining hours, shuttle timing, events, and quick links all existed, but they were distributed across separate systems with different patterns and different levels of urgency.

That meant students spent time checking for information rather than using it. The opportunity was not to add more features, but to centralize the everyday layer of campus life into one place that could be scanned in seconds.

03

Research and ideation

Before I designed the interface, I looked at how students already stitched together their NYU routines. The process was simple but revealing: interview notes from real tool usage, and then a small competitive scan of university dashboards to see what felt useful, what felt outdated, and what was still missing.

That process pushed the concept in a clear direction. The product needed to reduce switching between tools, bring the most time-sensitive information closer to the surface, and let people customize the dashboard around how they actually move through campus.

Method 01

Interview notes

Finding 01

Shuttle tracking was the clearest pain point.

The student I interviewed already used Passio GO for the NYU shuttle, but described it as inconvenient and UI-unfriendly. Real-time information mattered, but the current experience made checking it feel frustrating.

Finding 02

Daily scheduling had already moved to Google Calendar.

Instead of relying on Albert for everyday reference, the schedule had been exported into Google Calendar. That showed me students were already building their own smoother system when official tools did not support daily use well.

Finding 03

Email was official, but hard to scan.

Important announcements, deadlines, and institutional updates existed, but they were buried inside noisy email flows that were not optimized for quick campus check-ins.

Finding 04

Opportunities felt scattered and easy to miss.

Research, jobs, and extracurricular events were all things the student cared about, but there was no clear central feed for finding them in one place.

Method 02

Competitive analysis

Reference 01

MyUCLA proved the value of one unified view.

Its strongest idea was putting news, events, schedule, and other student information in one place. Its main weakness was density. Everything was there, but the first glance felt heavy.

Reference 02

Trident's quadrant structure was intuitive, but dated.

I liked how quickly it separated classes, news, tools, and help. At the same time, the heavy borders, clipart-like icons, and old visual rhythm made it feel stuck in an earlier web pattern.

Reference 03

Squiz was cleaner, but still incomplete.

Its layout felt more organized and balanced, but it still lacked the most relevant needs from the interview: stronger assignment context, shuttle tracking, and a better opportunity feed.

Direction

What I carried forward

I wanted the clarity of a modular dashboard without the visual heaviness or content gaps I saw elsewhere. That became the basis for the final widget-based system.

04

Paper prototype and feedback

I translated the dashboard into a quick paper prototype before refining the interface digitally. That made it easier to test structure, priorities, and whether the screen already felt useful without relying on visual polish.

Testing with two users confirmed that customization was one of the strongest parts of the concept. Choosing widgets made the dashboard feel more personal and more useful right away.

The main weakness was clickability. Some buttons and links felt too quiet, so the next version focused on making actions easier to recognize at a glance.

05

System concept

Once the process clarified what students actually needed, the final system direction became more focused: one home screen, modular widgets, and a responsive structure that could adapt from desktop overview to mobile check-in. Personalization was treated as part of the product logic, not just a preference layer.

The widget system let the dashboard stay broad enough for campus life without becoming visually overloaded. Students could opt into what mattered most to them and ignore the rest, while the layout still kept the overall interface organized.

06

Selected prototype moments

The final prototype focused on the moments that carry the concept best: setup, everyday dashboard use, and later customization. Together they show how the dashboard moves from generic campus information to a personal daily tool.

07

Design principles

The visual system stayed intentionally restrained. I used NYU violet, straight dividers, white space, and clear typography so the interface felt institutional but still calm. The strongest principle was efficiency: show more context without making the screen feel busy.

08

Reflection

This was one of my first UI-focused UX projects, and it made me realize how much I enjoy validating an idea, refining it through feedback, and iterating until the structure feels obvious. The process taught me that interface design is not just about arranging content beautifully. It is about deciding what deserves to be seen first.

Looking back, the most valuable part was seeing how each step changed the concept: the interview sharpened the problem, the competitive analysis clarified what to avoid, and the paper prototype made weak affordances visible before I invested too much in polish.

If I continued the project, I would test the dashboard with students from different schools and routines, push the personalization model further, and explore how the system could adapt across semester, exam, and travel periods.

The project became stronger once the process stopped being about making one more portal and started being about reducing the friction of everyday student life.
